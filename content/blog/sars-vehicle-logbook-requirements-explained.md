If you use a car for work in South Africa — whether you own it, lease it, or receive a travel allowance — SARS will eventually ask how much of your driving was business versus private. The answer is not a guess at year-end. It is a logbook: dated trips, odometer readings, destinations, and purposes that support the percentage of vehicle costs you claim on your tax return.

This guide explains SARS vehicle logbook requirements in plain language. It is not tax advice. Allowance rules, deemed cost methods, and employer policies vary. Confirm your situation with SARS publications, your employer, or a registered tax practitioner.

## Why SARS cares about your logbook

Vehicle expenses are high on the list of disputed deductions because the same car usually serves both business and personal life. SARS allows claims for business use, not for commuting from home to a regular workplace in most cases, and not for private errands disguised as work trips.

A logbook is your evidence. Without it, you may have to fall back on less favourable methods, reduce your claim, or defend an assessment with weak documentation. For employees with travel allowances, an incomplete logbook can mean the allowance is fully taxed. For sole proprietors, it affects wear-and-tear, fuel, insurance, and maintenance apportionment.

Think of the logbook as part of your financial hygiene — like keeping invoices for [client billing](/invoicefast). It is tedious in small moments and valuable in big ones.

## Who needs a vehicle logbook

You typically need consistent trip records if you:

- Receive a travel allowance and want to claim against it on your ITR12
- Are self-employed and deduct vehicle costs apportioned to business use
- Use a company car and must substantiate private versus business kilometres
- Claim actual costs rather than the simplified deemed cost tables

Even if you use SARS deemed cost tables for some elements, business kilometres as a percentage of total kilometres still drives the calculation. That percentage comes from your logbook.

## What each trip record should include

SARS expects a logbook to show enough detail that an assessor can verify business travel. For each business trip, record:

- Date of travel
- Opening odometer reading (or cumulative km at trip start)
- Closing odometer reading (or km at trip end)
- Kilometres travelled for that trip
- Business purpose — who you visited, what work was performed, or why the trip was necessary
- Destination — suburb, client name, or site is usually sufficient

Private trips should be recorded too, or at least reflected in your total km for the period. You calculate business use as business km divided by total km. Omitting private driving inflates your percentage and creates audit risk.

Odometer-based recording beats estimating distances from Google Maps after the fact. Assessors treat contemporaneous records more seriously than reconstructed spreadsheets in April.

## The 12-month logbook rule

For many allowance and company-car scenarios, SARS requires a logbook covering a continuous twelve-month period that is representative of your travel pattern. That period does not have to align with the tax year, but it must be complete — gaps undermine credibility.

If your work pattern changes dramatically — new job, fully remote, second office — you may need a fresh representative period. A logbook from a year when you drove to clients daily does not support a year when you mostly work from home.

Start the habit early in the tax year or allowance cycle. Reconstructing twelve months from fuel slips alone is painful.

## Business vs private: trips that trip people up

Common mistakes include:

- Logging home-to-office commuting as business travel when it is a fixed place of employment
- Labeling shopping or school runs as "business" without a genuine work purpose
- Using round numbers for every trip (exactly 50 km, week after week)
- Missing odometer readings and only listing destinations
- Keeping a logbook for two months and extrapolating for ten

Legitimate business trips often include visiting clients, travelling between multiple work sites in one day, or attending meetings away from your regular base. When purpose is genuine, write it clearly.

If you are unsure whether a trip qualifies, note it honestly and ask your practitioner at year-end.

## Supporting documents beyond the logbook

A logbook works best alongside:

- Fuel and toll receipts
- Service and insurance invoices for the vehicle
- Proof of ownership or lease agreement
- Employer travel allowance on your IRP5, if applicable

The logbook provides the km split; supporting docs provide the cost base. Both matter.

## Monthly vs annual habits

Waiting until February to fill twelve months of trips is the most common failure mode. Monthly review takes minutes:

- Confirm every business trip has odometer readings
- Reconcile total km against your service odometer photo or dashboard reading
- Flag private trips you forgot to log

Annual export should be a summary your accountant can use — CSV or PDF with totals for business km, private km, and percentage business use.

## Employer travel allowances and IRP5 codes

Employees face an extra layer: your IRP5 shows whether you received a travel allowance and under which code. Your logbook percentage interacts with those codes to determine taxable versus deductible portions. Payroll and tax return must tell the same story.

If your employer reimburses km at a fixed rate, understand whether that is an allowance subject to logbook rules or a reimbursement structure with different treatment. Mixing them up on the ITR12 triggers auto-assessments.

## Company cars and fringe benefits

A company car carries fringe benefit values on payroll, while your logbook still matters for business/private splits in specific arrangements. Company car rules are specialised — get employer payroll guidance and practitioner sign-off if this applies to you.

## What happens if SARS queries your claim

SARS may request the logbook and supporting documents for a specific year. Queries often focus on business percentage seeming too high for the occupation, missing odometer continuity, or business purposes that do not match client records.

Respond promptly with the representative period and summaries SARS asks for.

## Simple checklist before you file

- Twelve consecutive months recorded (or current year-to-date if mid-cycle and your practitioner agrees)
- Every business trip has date, odometer, km, destination, and purpose
- Private travel reflected in total km
- Business use percentage calculated and noted
- Fuel and maintenance records stored by tax year
- Export saved where your accountant expects it
