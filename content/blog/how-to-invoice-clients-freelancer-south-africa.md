Sending your first invoice as a freelancer in South Africa can feel more intimidating than doing the actual work. You want to look professional, satisfy your client's accounts payable team, and stay on the right side of SARS if VAT applies — without spending an afternoon fighting Word templates.

This guide walks through what to put on an invoice, how payment terms work in practice, and habits that reduce chasing late payments. It is general business information, not legal or tax advice. When VAT registration, withholding, or cross-border rules apply, confirm details with a practitioner.

## Before you invoice: quotes, contracts, and scope

An invoice is not a surprise. It should match what the client already agreed to — a signed quote, proposal, statement of work, or email approval with clear deliverables and price. South African clients, especially corporates, often require a purchase order number before they pay.

Minimum pre-invoice checklist:

- Client legal name and billing address (Pty Ltd name, not only a contact's nickname)
- Your name or trading name and contact details
- Description of work tied to deliverables or milestones
- Agreed fee: fixed project, hourly, retainer, or day rate
- Whether the price is VAT exclusive or inclusive (if you are VAT registered)
- Payment terms: due on receipt, 7 days, 30 days, or milestone-based

If scope crept mid-project, confirm a change order in writing before invoicing the extra. Disputed invoices sit unpaid while negotiations reopen.

## What every freelancer invoice should include

Even without VAT registration, a professional invoice helps clients pay you and helps you at tax time. Include:

- The word "Invoice" (and "Tax Invoice" if VAT registered — see below)
- A unique invoice number — sequential numbering is standard
- Invoice date and due date
- Your full name or business name, address, email, and phone
- Client name and billing address
- Line items: description, quantity or hours, rate, line total
- Subtotal, any VAT, and total amount due
- Payment instructions: bank name, account holder, account number, branch code, and payment reference
- Optional: company registration number if you trade under a registered name

For VAT vendors, SARS requires additional fields on tax invoices above certain amounts — including your VAT number and the client's VAT number if they are registered vendors. Missing VAT numbers delay corporate clients who need input tax claims.

## VAT exclusive vs inclusive pricing

South African B2B quoting is usually VAT exclusive with VAT shown as a separate line. If you quote "R10 000" to a corporate client, clarify whether that is excl. or incl. VAT. Ambiguity causes rejected invoices and awkward reissues.

If you are not VAT registered, your invoice has no VAT line — the total is the total. Do not add a fake VAT line "for appearance." Once you register, update every template and re-quote active projects if needed.

## Payment terms that actually get you paid

"Due on receipt" sounds assertive but corporates often default to 30 or 60 days regardless. Align terms to reality:

- Ask about AP cycles before you start — some clients only run payments on fixed Fridays
- Request a deposit on new clients or large projects (30–50% is common in creative and dev work)
- Tie milestone invoices to delivered artefacts, not vague "Phase 2 complete" wording
- Put late-interest clauses in your contract; you may never enforce them, but they signal seriousness

Your invoice reference field matters. Ask the client what their AP system needs — PO number, project code, or supplier number. Wrong references bounce invoices back silently.

## Bank details and payment safety

Publish your banking details only on invoices and direct client comms — not on public websites where they can be scraped for fraud. If you change bank accounts, notify clients through a verified channel, not a lone email that could be spoofed.

For international clients paying in foreign currency, state whether you accept SWIFT, Wise, PayPal, or other rails, and who absorbs conversion fees. ZAR freelancers often quote in Rands with a note that the client's bank converts at their rate.

## Credit notes, deposits, and partial payments

When you refund or discount after invoicing, issue a credit note referencing the original invoice number. Keep the audit trail clean for both your income tax and VAT records.

Deposits should be invoiced as deposits — with final invoice balancing the remainder. Calling a deposit "Invoice 1" and never issuing "Invoice 2" confuses everyone at year-end.

## Pro forma vs tax invoice

A pro forma invoice is a quote or prepayment request — not a tax document for input VAT. Convert to a proper tax invoice when the supply occurs and payment terms crystallise. VAT vendors especially should not treat pro formas as substitutes for tax invoices on the date of supply.

## Record-keeping for SARS and your accountant

Keep copies of every invoice issued, every payment received, and every expense receipt related to the project. SARS expects records for at least five years. Your annual ITR12 as a sole proprietor reports gross income and allowable expenses — invoices are the backbone of gross income proof.

Organise by tax year (1 March to 28/29 February for individuals). Match invoice dates to bank deposits. If a client pays in January for December work, your practitioner will allocate timing; your records should show both the invoice and the deposit clearly.

## Chasing overdue invoices professionally

A polite rhythm works better than silence followed by anger:

- Day after due date: friendly reminder with PDF reattached
- One week: note outstanding reference and ask if AP needs anything
- Two weeks: escalate to your contact's manager or finance lead
- Beyond terms in contract: pause new work until resolved

Document every chase. If you later write off bad debt, your records support the decision.
